Three Properties of a Mind

Initiative — Discipline — Interference. After Hodges, Alan Turing: The Enigma, Chapter 6 'Mercury Delayed'.

Turing argued in Intelligent Machinery (1948) that initiative, discipline, and interference are the three properties that distinguish a merely mechanical system from a potentially intelligent one. Hodges reconstructs this argument in Chapter 6. Move the sliders to explore the space of possible minds.

Scenario II — Initiative in Arithmetic

Scenario II — Initiative in arithmetic

Gauss, as a child, was asked to sum a sequence of numbers. He found a shortcut no one had taught him. Turing used this as his example of initiative — doing something correct that was not in the instructions.

"It is related that the infant Gauss was asked at school to do the addition 15+18+21+...+54 and that he immediately wrote down 483, presumably having calculated it as (15+54)(54−12)/2,3... One can imagine a situation where the children were given a number of additions to do, of which the first 5 were all arithmetic progressions, but the 6th was say 23+34+45...+100+112+122...+199. Gauss might have given the answer to this as if it were an arithmetic progression, not having noticed that the 9th term was 112 instead of 111. This would be a definite mistake, which the less intelligent children would not have been likely to make." — Hodges, Alan Turing: The Enigma, p. 476
Scenario III — The Obedience Failure

Scenario III — The obedience failure

A fully disciplined machine does not halt when given a wrong instruction — it obeys perfectly. Turing described the educated machine as like a person who 'would have no common sense, and would obey the most ridiculous orders unflinchingly.' This is a different failure from freezing.

"If the object were simply to produce a universal machine, it would be better to design and build it directly. The point, however, was that the machine thus educated would not only acquire the capacity to carry out complicated instructions, which Alan described as like a person who 'would have no common sense, and would obey the most ridiculous orders unflinchingly'. It would not only be able to do its duty, but would have that elusive 'initiative' that characterised intelligence." — Hodges, Alan Turing: The Enigma, p. 478
